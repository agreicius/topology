    Section 1.13 Free products

    Definition 1.13.1. Free product of groups.

    Let \(\{G_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}\) be a family of groups. A free product of the groups \(G_\alpha\) is a pair \((G, \{i_\alpha\colon G_\alpha\rightarrow G\})\text{,}\) consisting of a group \(G\) and a family of group homomorphisms \(i_\alpha\colon G_\alpha\rightarrow G\text{,}\) that satisfies the following universal mapping property:
    • Universal mapping property.
      If \(H\) is any group, and if \(\{\phi_\alpha\colon G_\alpha\rightarrow H\}_{\alpha\in I}\) is any family of group homomorphisms, then there is a unique group homomorphism \(\phi\colon G\rightarrow H\) satisfying
      \begin{equation*} i_\alpha\circ\phi=\phi_\alpha \end{equation*}
      for all \(\alpha\in I\text{.}\)
    As we will see, such a group \(G\) is unique up to isomorphism. We will write \(\prod_{\alpha\in I}^*G_\alpha\) to denote a free product of the \(G_\alpha\text{.}\) When \(I=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\}\) is finite, we write \(G_{\alpha_1}*G_{\alpha_2}*\cdots *G_{\alpha_n}\) to denote a free product of the \(G_{\alpha_k}\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.13.2. Free product properties.

    Let \(\{G_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}\) be a family of groups.
    1. If \((G,\{i_\alpha\colon G_\alpha\colon G\})\) is a free product, then \(i_\alpha\) is injective for all \(\alpha\in I\text{.}\)
    2. If \((G, \{i_\alpha\colon G_\alpha\rightarrow G)\})\) and \((G', \{i_\alpha'\colon G_\alpha\rightarrow G'\})\) are both free products of the \(G_\alpha\text{,}\) then \(G\cong G'\text{.}\)
    We now endeavor to show that free products of groups exist. Essentially, we will build a group \(G\cong \prod\limits_{\alpha\in I}^* G_\alpha\) whose elements consist of reduced words on the alphabet \(\coprod G_\alpha\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.13.3. Words and reduced words.

    Let \(\{G_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}\) be a family of groups, and let \(\mathcal{A}=\coprod G_\alpha\) be the disjoint union of their underlying sets. Given an integer \(n\geq 0\text{,}\) a word of length \(n\) on the \alphabet \(\mathcal{A}\) is an \(n\)-tuple \(w=(g_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n})\) where \(g_{\alpha_k}\in G_{\alpha_k}\) for all \(1\leq k\leq n\text{.}\) The empty word is the empty sequence \(()\text{,}\) the unique tuple in \(\mathcal{A}\) of length 0.
    A word \(w\) is reduced if either \(w=()\) is empty, or \(w=(g_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n})\text{,}\) where \(g_{\alpha_k}\ne e_{\alpha_k}\) for all \(1\leq k\leq n\) and \(\alpha_{k}\ne \alpha_{k+1}\) for all \(1\leq k\leq n-1\text{.}\)
    We could attempt to define the free product of a family \(\{G_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}\) as the set \(W\) of all reduced words on the alphabet \(\mathcal{A}=\coprod G_\alpha\text{,}\) where given reduced words \(w=(g_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n})\) and \(u=(h_{\beta_1},h_{\beta_2},\dots, h_{beta_m})\) we define their product \(w*u\) to be the result of ‚Äúsimplifying‚Äù the concatenated word \((g_{\alpha_1},\dots, g_{\alpha_n}, h_{\beta_1},\dots, h_{\beta_m})\) by repeatedly ‚Äúfusing‚Äù any two adjacent terms that happen to live in the same group \(G_\alpha\subseteq \mathcal{A}\text{.}\) This is a fairly concrete construction, but the proof that this operation is associative is a nightmare! The construction in the proof below is a sneaky way of avoiding this hassle, often called the trick of van der Waerden.

    Theorem 1.13.4. Free products exist.

    Given a family of groups \(\{G_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}\text{,}\) a free product \(G\cong \prod\limits_{\alpha\in I}^*G_\alpha\) exists.

    Proof.

    Let \(\mathcal{A}\) be the disjoint union of the \(G_\alpha\text{,}\) let \(W\) be the set of all reduced words on \(\mathcal{A}\text{,}\) and let \(S_W\) be the set of all permutations of \(W\text{.}\) In other words, \(S_W\) is the set of all bijections \(\sigma\colon W\rightarrow W\text{,}\) a group under function composition.
    Fix \(\alpha\in I\text{.}\) We now define a map \(i_\alpha G_\alpha\rightarrow S_W\) that associates to each element \(g\in G_\alpha\) a permutation \(\sigma_g\colon W\rightarrow W\in S_W\text{.}\) The permutation \(\sigma_g=i_\alpha(g)\) is defined as follows:
    \begin{align*} \sigma_e \amp =\id_W \\ \sigma_g((g_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n}))\amp =\begin{cases} (g) \amp \text{if } n=0 \\ (g,g_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n}) \amp \text{if } n\ne 0 \text{ and } \alpha\ne \alpha_1 \\ (gg_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n})\amp \text{if } n\ne 0 \text{ and } \alpha=\alpha_1 \text{ and } g_{\alpha_1}\ne g^{-1} \\ (g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n}) \amp \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \amp (\text{for } g\ne e) \end{align*}
    It is now straightforward, if somewhat tedious, to show that \(\sigma_{gh}=\sigma_g\circ \sigma_{h}\) for all \(g,h\in G_\alpha\text{,}\) whence it follows that \(\sigma_g\) is indeed a bijection with inverse \(\sigma_g^{-1}=\sigma_{g^{-1}\text{,}\) and finally that \(i_\alpha\colon G_\alpha\rightarrow S_W\) is a group homomorphism.

    Theorem 1.13.5. Products of free products.

    If \(G=\prod\limits_{\alpha\in I}^* G_\alpha\) and \(H\cong \prod\limits_{\alpha'\in J}^*G_{\alpha'}\text{,}\) then \(G*H\cong \prod\limits_{\alpha\in I\cup J}^* G_\alpha\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.13.6. Least normal subgroup.

    Let \(S\) be a subset of the group \(G\text{.}\) The least normal subgroup containing \(S\) is the intersection of all normal subgroups of \(G\) that contain \(S\text{.}\) Equivalently, the least normal subgroup containing \(S\) is the unique normal subgroup \(N\normalin G\) such that (i) \(S\subseteq N\) and (ii) if \(S\subseteq N'\normalin G\text{,}\) then \(N\subseteq N'\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.13.7. Quotients of free products.

    Let \(G=G_1*G_2\) with accompanying maps \(i_k\colon G_k\rightarrow G\text{,}\) \(1\leq k\leq 2\text{.}\) For each \(1\leq k\leq 2\text{,}\) we identify \(G_k\) with its image \(i_k(G_k)\leq G\text{.}\) Given normal subgroups \(N_k\normalin G_k\) for \(k\in \{1,2\}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} G_1/N_1 * G_2/N_2\cong G/N\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(N\normalin G\) is the smallest normal subgroup of \(G\) containing \(N_1\) and \(N_2\text{.}\)
