We show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is perfect. We know already that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed. Given any \(x\in \mathcal{C}\) and \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) pick \(N\in \Z_+\) such that \(1/3^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\in C_{N}\text{,}\) we have \(x\in I=[a_k/3^N, (a_k+1)/3^N]\) for some \(a_k\in \{0,1,\dots, 3^N-1\}\text{.}\) Let \(J, K\) be the two subintervals of \(I\) remaining after deleting its middle third. Assume without loss of generality that \(x\in J\text{.}\) The collection \(\{C_n\cap K\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is itself a family of nested, closed and nonempty subsets of \([0,1]\text{.}\) Again by compactness, there is an element \(y\in \bigcap_{n=0}^\infty (C_n\cap K)\subseteq \mathcal{C}\text{.}\) Since \(x,y\in I\text{,}\) we have \(d(x,y)\leq 1/3^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\in J\) and \(y\in K\text{,}\) and since \(J\cap K=\emptyset\text{,}\) we have \(x\ne y\text{.}\)
