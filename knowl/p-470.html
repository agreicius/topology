We could attempt to define the free product of a family \(\{G_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}\) as the set \(W\) of all reduced words on the alphabet \(\mathcal{A}=\coprod G_\alpha\text{,}\) where given reduced words \(w=(g_{\alpha_1},g_{\alpha_2},\dots, g_{\alpha_n})\) and \(u=(h_{\beta_1},h_{\beta_2},\dots, h_{beta_m})\) we define their product \(w*u\) to be the result of “simplifying” the concatenated word \((g_{\alpha_1},\dots, g_{\alpha_n}, h_{\beta_1},\dots, h_{\beta_m})\) by repeatedly “fusing” any two adjacent terms that happen to live in the same group \(G_\alpha\subseteq \mathcal{A}\text{.}\) This is a fairly concrete construction, but the proof that this operation is associative is a nightmare! The construction in the proof below is a sneaky way of avoiding this hassle, often called the trick of van der Waerden.
