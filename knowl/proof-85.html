Proof

It is clear that (2) implies (1): for any \(x\in X\text{,}\) taking \(U=X\text{,}\) we get an open neighborhood \(V\ni x\) such that \(\overline{V}\) is compact. Thus \(K\) is a compact neighborhood of \(x\text{.}\)
We now show (1) implies (2). Munkres' proof elegantly uses the one-point compactification of \(X\text{.}\) Solely by way of contrast I proceed more directly. Fix \(x\in X\) and open neighborhood \(U\ni x\text{.}\) Since \(X\) is locally compact, we have \(x\in U'\subseteq K\) for some open set \(U'\) and compact set \(K\text{.}\) Taking \(U'\cap U\) we may assume further that \(U'\subseteq U\text{.}\) The set \(C=K-U\) is a closed subset of the compact set \(K\text{,}\) hence compact. Since \(X\) is Hausdorff, we can find disjoint open sets \(V', W'\) containing \(x\) and \(C\text{,}\) respectively. (See TheoremÂ 2.15.7.) Let \(V=V'\cap U\text{.}\) Since \(\overline{V}\subseteq K\) is a closed subset of a compact set, it is compact. Furthermore, since \(\overline{V}\) is disjoint from \(C=K-U\text{,}\) we must have \(V\subseteq\overline{V}\subseteq U\text{.}\)
