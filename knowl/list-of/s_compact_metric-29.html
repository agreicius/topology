Topological specimen 16. Cantor set.

There is a unique family \(\{C_n\}_{n=0}^\infty\) of closed sets of \([0,1]\) of of \([0,1]\) satisfying the following properties:
  • \(C_0=[0,1]\text{.}\)
  • \(C_n=\bigcup_{k=1}^{2^n}I_{n,k}\) is a disjoint union of \(2^n\) intervals of the form \([a_k/3^n, (a_k+1)/3^n\text{,}\) where \(a_k\in \{0,1,\dots, 3^n-1\}\text{.}\)
  • \(C_{n+1}\) is obtained by “deleting the middle third” of each interval appearing in \(C_n\text{:}\) that is each interval \([a_k/3^n, (a_k+1)/3^n]\) of \(C_n\) gives rise to two intervals \([a_{k}/3^{n+1}, (a_k+1)/3^{n+1}]\) and \([(a_k+2)/3^{n+1}, (a_k+3)/3^{n+1}]\text{.}\)
Here is a tree illustration of the first three members of this family. The Cantor set is defined as \(\mathcal{C}=\bigcap_{n=0}^\infty C_n\text{.}\)
The Cantor set is nonempty, (i) Hausdorff, (ii) compact, (iii) perfect, (iv) totally disconnected, and (v) second countable, as we show below. Amazingly, you can also show that any nonempty space \(X\) satisfying properties \((i)-(v)\) is homeomorphic to the Cantor set!

Proof.

Since \([0,1]\) is compact, and since \(\{C_n\}_{n=0}^\infty\) is a collection of nested nonempty closed sets, we know by Corollary 1.14.14 that \(\mathcal{C}\ne \emptyset\text{.}\) Since each \(C_n\) is clearly closed, \(\mathcal{C}=\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty C_n\) is closed and bounded, hence compact. Since \(\mathcal{C}\) is a subspace of a Hausdorff space, it is Hausdorff.
We show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is perfect. We know already that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed. Given any \(x\in \mathcal{C}\) and \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) pick \(N\in \Z_+\) such that \(1/3^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\in C_{N}\text{,}\) we have \(x\in I=[a_k/3^N, (a_k+1)/3^N]\) for some \(a_k\in \{0,1,\dots, 3^N-1\}\text{.}\) Let \(J, K\) be the two subintervals of \(I\) remaining after deleting its middle third. Assume without loss of generality that \(x\in J\text{.}\) The collection \(\{C_n\cap K\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is itself a family of nested, closed and nonempty subsets of \([0,1]\text{.}\) Again by compactness, there is an element \(y\in \bigcap_{n=0}^\infty (C_n\cap K)\subseteq \mathcal{C}\text{.}\) Since \(x,y\in I\text{,}\) we have \(d(x,y)\leq 1/3^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\in J\) and \(y\in K\text{,}\) and since \(J\cap K=\emptyset\text{,}\) we have \(x\ne y\text{.}\)
We prove \(\mathcal{C}\) is totally disconnected. Let \(A\) be any subset of \(\mathcal{C}\) containing two distinct elements \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) We will find a nontrivial separation of \(A\text{.}\) Since \(x\ne y\text{,}\) we can find an \(n\) and disjoint intervals \(I_{n,j}=[a_j/3^n, (a_j+1)/3^n], I_{n,k}=[a_k/3^n, (a_k+1)/3^n]\) such that \(x\in I_{n,j}\) and \(y\in I_{n,k}\text{:}\) otherwise we would have \(d(x,y)\leq 1/3^n\) for all \(n\text{,}\) which would imply \(d(x,y)=0\text{.}\) Assume without loss of generality that \(a_j< a_k\text{,}\) and let \(b\) be any element between \(a_j\) and \(a_k\) that does not lie in \(C_n\text{.}\) The open sets \(U=A\cap [0,b)\) and \(V=A\cap (b,1]\) form a separation of \(A\text{,}\) proving it is not connected. We conclude that the only connected subsets of \(\mathcal{C}\) are singletons, and thus that the connected components are singletons.
