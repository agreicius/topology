Proof

Since \([0,1]\) is compact, and since \(\{C_n\}_{n=0}^\infty\) is a collection of nested nonempty closed sets, we know by CorollaryÂ 2.15.14 that \(\mathcal{C}\ne \emptyset\text{.}\) Since each \(C_n\) is clearly closed, \(\mathcal{C}=\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty C_n\) is closed and bounded, hence compact. Since \(\mathcal{C}\) is a subspace of a Hausdorff space, it is Hausdorff.
We show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is perfect. We know already that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed. Given any \(x\in \mathcal{C}\) and \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) pick \(N\in \Z_+\) such that \(1/3^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\in C_{N}\text{,}\) we have \(x\in I=[a_k/3^N, (a_k+1)/3^N]\) for some \(a_k\in \{0,1,\dots, 3^N-1\}\text{.}\) Let \(J, K\) be the two subintervals of \(I\) remaining after deleting its middle third. Assume without loss of generality that \(x\in J\text{.}\) The collection \(\{C_n\cap K\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is itself a family of nested, closed and nonempty subsets of \([0,1]\text{.}\) Again by compactness, there is an element \(y\in \bigcap_{n=0}^\infty (C_n\cap K)\subseteq \mathcal{C}\text{.}\) Since \(x,y\in I\text{,}\) we have \(d(x,y)\leq 1/3^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\in J\) and \(y\in K\text{,}\) and since \(J\cap K=\emptyset\text{,}\) we have \(x\ne y\text{.}\)
We prove \(\mathcal{C}\) is totally disconnected. Let \(A\) be any subset of \(\mathcal{C}\) containing two distinct elements \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) We will find a separation of \(A\text{.}\) Since \(x\ne y\text{,}\) we can find an \(n\) and disjoint intervals \(I_{n,j}=[a_j/3^n, (a_j+1)/3^n], I_{n,k}=[a_k/3^n, (a_k+1)/3^n]\) such that \(x\in I_{n,j}\) and \(y\in I_{n,k}\text{:}\) otherwise we would have \(d(x,y)\leq 1/3^n\) for all \(n\text{,}\) which would imply \(d(x,y)=0\text{.}\) Assume without loss of generality that \(a_j< a_k\text{,}\) and let \(b\) be any element between \(a_j\) and \(a_k\) that does not lie in \(C_n\text{.}\) The open sets \(U=A\cap [0,b)\) and \(V=A\cap (b,1]\) form a separation of \(A\text{,}\) proving it is not connected. We conclude that the only connected subsets of \(\mathcal{C}\) are singletons, and thus that the connected components are singletons.
