Proof

Let \(\mathcal{C}\subseteq [0,1]\) be the Cantor set, and choose a homeomorphism \(h\colon \mathcal{C}\rightarrow \{0,1\}^\omega\text{,}\) where \(\{0,1\}\) is given the discrete topology and \(\{0,1\}^\omega\) is given the product topology. (We proved \(\mathcal{C}\) is homeomorphic to \(\{0,1\}^\omega\) in discussion.) Now define functions \(g_1,g_2\colon \{0,1\}\rightarrow [0,1]\) as
\begin{align*} g_1((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots))\amp= \frac{a_1}{2}+\frac{a_3}{2^2}+\frac{a_5}{2^3}+\cdots =\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{a_{2k-1}}{2^k} \\ g_2((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots))\amp= \frac{a_2}{2}+\frac{a_4}{2^2}+\frac{a_6}{2^3}+\cdots =\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{a_{2k}}{2^k} \end{align*}
Both functions \(g_i\) are easily seen to be continuous; we prove this for \(g_1\text{.}\) Let \(x=(a_1,a_2,\dots, )\) and let \(y=\sum_{k=1}a_{2k-1}/2^k\text{.}\) For any \(\epsilon > 0\text{,}\) pick \(N\) such that \(1/2^N< \epsilon\text{.}\) Let
\begin{equation*} U=\{a_1\}\times \{a_2\}\times \cdots\times \{a_N\}\times \prod_{k=N+1}^\infty\{0,1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
For any \(x'\in U\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{y-f(x')}=\abs{\sum_{k=N+1}^\infty \frac{a_{2k-1}}{2^k}}\leq \sum_{k=N+1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^k}=\frac{1}{2^N}< \epsilon\text{,} \end{equation*}
as desired.
Now using our homeomorphism \(h\colon \mathcal{C}\rightarrow \{0,1\}^\omega\text{,}\) we see that the functions \(f_1=g_1\circ h\) and \(f_2=g_2\circ h\) are continuous functions from \(\mathcal{C}\) to \([0,1]\text{.}\) By the Tietze extension theorem, we obtain continuous extensions \(\widetilde{f}_1\colon [0,1]\rightarrow [0,1]\) and \(\widetilde{f}_2\colon [0,1]\rightarrow [0,1]\text{.}\) The function \(\phi\colon [0,1]\rightarrow [0,1]\times [0,1]\) defined as \(f(x)=(\widetilde{f}_1(x), \widetilde{f}_2(x))\) is then a continuous function to \([0,1]\times[0,1]\text{.}\)
It remains only to show that \(\phi\) is surjective. Given any \((y_1,y_2)\in [0,1]\text{,}\) let \(y_1=\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{a_k}{2^k}\) and \(y_2=\sum_{k=1}\frac{b_k}{2^k}\) be binary expansions of \(y_1\) and \(y_2\text{.}\) Let \(s=(a_1,b_1,a_2,b_2,a_3,b_3,\dots)\text{,}\) and let \(x\in \mathcal{C}=h^{-1}(s)\text{.}\) We have
\begin{align*} \phi(x) \amp =(\widetilde{f}_1(x),\widetilde{f}_2(x))\\ \amp =(f_1(x),f_2(x)) \amp (x\in \mathcal{C})\\ \amp =(g_1(h(x)), g_2(h(x))) \amp (\text{def. of } f_i)\\ \amp =(g_1(s), g_2(s)) \amp (h(x)=s)\\ \amp =(y_1,y_2) \amp \text{(by construction)}\text{.} \end{align*}
This proves in fact that \(\phi\vert_\mathcal{C}\) is surjective.
