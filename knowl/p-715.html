Assume for that for all \(U\in \mathcal{T}\) and \(x\in U\) there is an element \(B\in \mathcal{B}\) such that \(x\in B\subseteq U\text{.}\) Taking \(U=X\) we see in particular that for all \(x\in X\) there is a \(B\in \mathcal{B}\) such that \(x\in B\text{.}\) Thus \(\mathcal{B}\) satisfies axiom (1) for a basis. Next, let \(B_1, B_2\in \mathcal{B}\) and take \(x\in B_1\cap B_2\text{.}\) Since \(B_1, B_2\in \mathcal{T}\) are open, so is \(U=B_1\cap B_2\text{.}\) Our assumption now implies that there is a \(B\in \mathcal{B}\) such that \(x\in B\subseteq U=B_1\cap B_2\text{.}\) Thus axiom (2) of DefinitionÂ 2.2.1 holds, and we condlude \(\mathcal{B}\) is a basis.
